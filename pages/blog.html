As the inaugural post on what began as a computer science portfolio and has gradually evolved into a more personal technical website, nothing felt more fitting than sharing two papers that have almost nothing to do with coding.

Presented here are two essays originally written in 2023, each centered on a foundational figure in the empiricist tradition: John Locke and David Hume.

The first examines Locke’s account of personal identity through the lens of a hypothetical medical procedure in which the transfer of consciousness replaces anaesthesia. The second explores Hume’s treatment of freedom and causal necessity, and his attempt to reconcile human liberty with determinism.

For those mercifully uninitiated into metaphysics, both Locke and Hume belong broadly to the empiricist tradition. In short, they argue that knowledge of the world originates in experience: observation, sensation, and the evidence provided by our senses.

This stands in contrast to the rationalist tradition, where experience is not considered a necessary precondition for knowledge. Much of this view follows from Descartes’ intuition-deduction thesis, which underpins not only his arguments for the existence of God, but also his broader account of knowledge and the external world (though that is probably another paper entirely).

Though far removed from operating systems, databases, or machine learning models, these papers reflect something that has increasingly shaped how I think about technology itself: questions of identity, causality, abstraction, and the limits of human understanding.
